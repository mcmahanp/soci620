The gausian model is $$ G_i\sim N(\mu,\sigma)\\ \mu \sim N(50,20)\\ \sigma \sim U(0,25) $$

In [3]:
m1 <- alist(
    latest_gini ~ dnorm(mu,sigma),
    mu ~ dnorm(50,20),
    sigma ~ dunif(0,25)
)
fit1 <- map(m1,data=d)
precis(fit1,prob=.9)

       Mean StdDev  5.0% 95.0%
mu    38.17   0.64 37.12 39.21
sigma  7.91   0.45  7.17  8.65

The posterior distribution for $\mu$ has a mean of about 38.17, and a 90% credible interval from about 37.12 to 39.21. The posterior distribution for $\sigma$ has a mean of about 7.91, and a 90% credible interval from about 7.17 to 8.65. Together, these results tell us that the average gini index predicted by our model is just below 40 with a spread of about 15 points in either direction. This agrees with the inexact results from question 1.

The updated model with colonized as a covariate is $$ G_i\sim N(\mu,\sigma)\\ \mu = \alpha + \beta_cC_i\\ \alpha \sim N(50,20)\\ \beta_c \sim N(0,20)\\ \sigma \sim U(0,25) $$

In [4]:
m2 <- alist(
    latest_gini ~ dnorm(mu,sigma),
    mu <- a + bc*colonized,
    a ~ dnorm(50,20),
    bc ~ dnorm(0,20),
    sigma ~ dunif(0,25)
)
fit2 <- map(m2,data=d)
precis(fit2,prob=.9)

       Mean StdDev  5.0% 95.0%
a     34.63   0.96 33.05 36.21
bc     5.73   1.22  3.72  7.74
sigma  7.39   0.42  6.70  8.08

This updated model estimates separate means for colonized and never-colonized countries. $\alpha$ is the mean for the never-colonized countries, the posterior for which has a mean of 34.63 and a 90% credible interval from about 33.05 to 36.21. This is lower than the estimate from the last model. The parmeter $\beta$ is difference in means between the colonized and never-colonized countries. The posterior distribution of $\beta$ has a mean of about 5.73 and a 90% credible interval from about 3.72 to 7.74. This means that the model predicts colonized countries will have Gini measures that are, on average, between about 3.72 and 7.74 higher than non-colonized countries. Colonized countries experience more inequality on average than non-colonized countries

m3 <- alist(
    latest_gini ~ dnorm(mu,sigma),
    mu <- a + b1*colonized + b2*population_pct_immigrant_2015,
    a ~ dnorm(50,30),
    b1 ~ dnorm(0,30),
    b2 ~ dnorm(0,30),
    sigma ~ dunif(0,25)
)
fit3 <- map(m3,data=d[!is.na(d$population_pct_immigrant_2015),])
precis(fit3)

       Mean StdDev  5.5% 94.5%
a     36.47   1.09 34.73 38.20
b1     5.16   1.20  3.24  7.08
b2    -0.24   0.08 -0.36 -0.12
sigma  7.16   0.41  6.50  7.81

The model above adds a covariate for the percent of a country's population that is foreign-born. The posterior for the coefficient on this new variable has a mean of about -0.24 and a 90% credible interval from about -0.36 to -.012. This means that for each percentage point increase in immigrant population in a country, the Gini coefficient for that country is predected to be about a quarter of a point lower. Put differently, if two countries had similar histories of colonization, but one had 20% imigrant population while the other had 10%, this model would predict that the country with more immigrants would have a gini index between about 1.2 and 3.6 points lower. Moreover, the coefficient on the colonized indicator is somewhat smaller (posterior mean of 5.16, 90% interval from 3.25 to 7.08). This suggest that a small portion of the difference in inequality between colonized and never-colonized countries could be due to average differences in immigrant populations in those countries. Finally, the coefficient on $\alpha$ has increased slightly (posterior mean 36.46, 90% interval 34.73 to 38.20. This is not surprising: all countries have at least some immigrant population, so the negative coefficient on immigrant population necesitates a lower coefficient on $\alpha$.
